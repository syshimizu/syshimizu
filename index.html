Senior Thesis in STEM Education

STEM education is an interdisciplinary major I created at Amherst College. The major encompasses a range of courses that focus on how and why we acquire knowledge, specifically in the domain of science, technology, engineering, and math. For my culminating senior thesis, I worked with Professor Kate Follette and her education research to investigate what factors contributed to demographic differences in quantitative literacy. Using various statistical methods and techniques, we found that psychological factors such as numerical efficacy and numerical self-efficacy were prominent factors in explaining gaps in quantitative literacy score. Here is my full thesis.

Reusable Drinkware Cleaner

In ENGS-21: Intro to Engineering, our team designed and fabricated a machine to clean reusable drinkware. To promote the use of reusables and eliminate disposables, we set out to develop an all-in-one system that cleans, dries, and sanitizes reusable drinkware in under 30 seconds. Throughout the 10 week term, we iterated over multiple designs, surveyed potential users, fabricated or machined various parts, and underwent various types of tests. While we were unable to implement all of our initial design specifications due to time and budget constraints, we were able to successfully create a machine that cleaned drinkware in under 30secs. Here is our full report.

Truss Bridge

In ENGS-33: Solid Mechanics, our team designed and constructed a truss bridge that aimed to minimize deflection and support a 1kN load. Design constraints included using less than 700cm3 volume of material, using a material other than plywood, and various size constraints. We decided on creating an arched truss bridge using solid ash for the main arches. While the truss design and construction were quite strong, our joinery is lacking especially at the base. Performance of our bridge would improve greatly with a thicker cross beam. Nevertheless, we were successfully able to support a 1kN load. Here is our full report.

Hall Labs

In the summer of 2018, I was fortunate to work in and learn about Professor David Hall's Bose Einstein Condensation lab. There were two main projects I was involved in: investigating the performance of a tapered amplifier (TA), and creating a temperature control circuit. The TA increases the power of an input laser beam, and our task was to measure and maximize it's output. While an ostensibly simple task, the set up was rather difficult and complicated, requiring much precision and fine-tuning of optical instruments. However, in the process we learned about various techniques and methods of manipulating lasers and functions of optical tools. My next project was to create a temperature control circuit to maintain the temperature of a negative current control box. The circuit was already designed; my task was mainly to construct the circuit, machine its housing, and test its performance.
I also designed a logo for Hall Labs for fun, incorporating the iconic hapless Alice expression :).
